# Cyber Security Guidelines



## Common Cyber Security Threats in a Remote Work Environment



Some common cyber security threats in a remote work environment include:



* Phishing emails designed to steal login credentials.

* Malicious links and attachments.

* Weak or reused passwords.

* Unsecured public Wi-Fi networks.

* Malware and ransomware attacks.

* Social engineering attacks where attackers impersonate trusted individuals.

* Data leaks caused by lost or stolen devices.



## Best Practices for Keeping Devices and Accounts Secure



* Use strong and unique passwords for every account.

* Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever possible.

* Keep operating systems and software updated.

* Use antivirus and endpoint protection software.

* Avoid downloading files from untrusted sources.

* Lock devices when away from your workspace.

* Regularly back up important data.

* Be cautious when opening links and attachments.



## Why Is It Important to Lock Your Computer When Away From Your Desk?



Locking a computer prevents unauthorized access to company and personal information. Even stepping away for a few minutes can create an opportunity for someone to access sensitive data, install malicious software, or misuse company accounts.



## How Should You Handle Phishing Attempts and Suspicious Links?



* Do not click suspicious links.

* Verify the sender's identity before responding.

* Check email addresses carefully for impersonation attempts.

* Report suspicious emails to the appropriate person or team.

* Delete phishing emails after reporting them.

* When unsure, verify through a separate communication channel.



## What Makes a Strong Password and Why Use a Password Manager?



A strong password should:



* Be at least 12–16 characters long.

* Contain a combination of letters, numbers, and symbols.

* Be unique for every account.

* Avoid personal information and common words.



A password manager helps generate, store, and manage unique passwords securely so users do not need to remember them individually.



## Why Is Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) Important?



Two-factor authentication adds an additional layer of security by requiring a second verification step beyond a password.



Even if a password is compromised, attackers are less likely to gain access without the second authentication factor.



2FA should be enabled on:



* Email accounts

* GitHub accounts

* Cloud storage accounts

* Banking and financial accounts

* Work-related platforms

# Reflection



## What Security Measures Do You Currently Follow and Where Can You Improve?



I currently use strong passwords for important accounts, keep my devices updated, and avoid opening suspicious links or attachments. I also use biometric authentication on my phone and laptop.



One area where I can improve is ensuring that every account has a unique password and enabling 2FA on all services that support it.



## How Can You Make Secure Behaviour a Habit Rather Than an Afterthought?



I can make security a habit by:



* Locking my devices whenever I leave my workspace.

* Regularly reviewing account security settings.

* Using a password manager consistently.

* Thinking carefully before clicking links or downloading files.

* Keeping software and operating systems updated.



## What Steps Will You Take to Ensure Your Passwords and Accounts Are Secure?



* Use a password manager to store unique passwords.

* Enable 2FA wherever available.

* Avoid password reuse across multiple accounts.

* Monitor account login activity.

* Update weak passwords immediately.



## What Would You Do If You Suspected a Security Breach or Suspicious Activity?



If I suspected a security breach, I would:



1. Immediately change the affected password.

2. Revoke active sessions where possible.

3. Enable or verify 2FA settings.

4. Scan my device for malware.

5. Report the incident to the appropriate team member or supervisor.

6. Monitor the account for any further suspicious activity.
